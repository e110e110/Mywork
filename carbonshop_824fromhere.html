Carbonshop

Created a One-Stop Green Shopping Platform

Overview

The average annual carbon footprint from food for a person in the United States is 2 tons, equivalent to the carbon sequestered by 110 tree seedlings grown for 10 years. However, few grocery shopping platforms inform and encourage users to make green purchasing decisions. Therefore, we designed a mobile application that makes everyday green purchase decisions easier than ever, with diverse environmental metrics for everyday grocery consumer products and a financial incentive program that rewards green purchase decisions.

My role includes design concept initialization, user research (exploratory & in-depth), strategy & design discussions, mid-fidelity prototyping & testing, high-fidelity prototyping & testing, and final design with its validation.

Problem Statement

Carbon emissions from the grocery industry associated with agriculture, shipping, and food processing and storage are a good proxy for many environmental issues. The purchase decisions we make every day at the grocery store can truly impact the environment. However, people are unaware of the importance of low-carbon consumption in daily life, and currently there’s no efficient way to help them make daily green purchasing decisions.

Research

User Interviews

We conducted 11 user interviews during the discovery phase of this project to gain a better understanding of users' grocery shopping habits. We mainly exploring what are the main determinants of their daily grocery shopping, what factors preventing them from buying environment-friendly products, and what possible incentives can be used to engage them into making more green decisions in their grocery shopping process.

According to our user interviews, we made a user journey map of daily online grocery shopping experiences to further find out pain points and improvement opportunities in this process. Stages of this online grocery shopping includes:

  • Consider what grocery products
  • Choose which applications/websites to use
  • Search for specific items
  • Compare and evaluate
  • Checkout

Research - Key TakeAway


After analyzing our user research findings, we decided to concentrate on these three pain points because they will influence the users' green decision.

  • People are unaware of the consequences of their carbon footprint.
    People felt hard to see big impacts from their small actions. They did not purchase green products as they could not receive instant benefits through their decisions. Current products lack carbon footprint information, and people have no idea about potential impacts.
  • People need approachable and engaging ways to participate in a low-carbon lifestyle.
    People express interests if the platform can provide an attractive way of having them join in. Also, raising awareness of environmental issues is a serious topic, and there should be approachable ways for them to understand and engage in relevant activities.
  • People can be encouraged to go grocery shopping with rewards.
    Prices are one of the main determinants while doing grocery shopping. Also, specific rewards, including discounts, free samples, and participating in green funds, can greatly attract people to buy green groceries.

Design Strategy



The primary goal of this project is to assist in making their green grocery shopping decision easier.

Information Architecture


With our design strategies, we started to create the information architecture.

Rationale:

  • Firstly, our application displays the users' accomplishments on the homepage to remind them of the impact they have had. Users may shop in different stores, so we integrated these stores into the homepage to help users to shop.
  • Shop is one of our main navigation because users need to see product information when shopping. Users can browse different products or search for specific products while shopping.
  • In users’ shopping carts, to draw users' attention to their impact on the environment, we will present customers with information on how much carbon emissions have been cut by their order this time compared to typical purchasing. Users can receive green points if their order has a lower carbon emissions.
  • To encourage people who are unconcerned about the environment to consider green shopping, reward plays an important role in motivating consumers to buy low-carbon goods. Users can see their environmental impact on this reward page and redeem their green points for trees or green products.
  • In the profile page, users can see their order history and their impact on the environment to summarize their purchases.

Design System


To gather our findings for further inspiration in both wireframing and high-fidelity prototyping, we created a moodboard.
Based on the moodboard above, we created a style tile. We found there is an agreement that most designs use green colors as well as plant icons to illustrate the sense of environment and ecosystem. Therefore, we decided to use a variety of green shades as our color palette. Our high-fidelity mockup mainly utilizes green and white colors to emphasize the concept of sustainability, nature, and health.

Hi-Fi Prototype


We implemented high-fidelity prototypes based on the style tile we initially decided. For the Hi-fi prototype, we designed pages for main flows including “homepage and product listing page” for users to search products and add to their carts, “product description”, “check-out and replacement product”, “profile”, “redeem and my forest page” - which covers most of sections on our application to make further user test sessions easier and accurate.


User Testing and Iteration

We conducted user testing with a Hi-fi prototype. We started revising our Hi-fi prototype based on feedback from the user testing session.

User Testing on UserTesting.com


To validate our final design, we conducted 12 unmoderated usability testing sessions on UserTesting.com to validate our high-fidelity prototypes. We set up a main scenario with different tasks with post-task questions to test if there are any usability issues we need to fix. We received both qualitative responses and quantitative data from the participants.

  • Task 1: Onboarding and set your carbon budget
  • Task 2: Replace a product before checkout
  • Task 3: Redeem your eco-points

Learnings


  • Iterations
    It is important to test the prototypes to know what users think and iterate for better user flow. Through our four rounds of user testing and iterations, we learned a lot from users’ feedback and managed to design for better user flow and achieve the product goal at the same time.
  • The Importance of Customization
    We found out that some users prefer to spend points to redeem goods, while others prefer to use points to redeem games. Also, it was interesting to find out people have different interpretations and understanding of carbon emissions and possible incentives. Therefore, it is important to think about motivating them in different ways.
  • Communication and Collaboration
    We spent most of our time online in group meetings on Zoom and collaborative designs on Figma. We learned how to communicate and utilize strengths from each other to work productively throughout the design process.

Next Steps


  • Create a quick tutorial for users
    Some users are unsure about how to earn and redeem points, so creating a tutorial can help users to know how to shop and redeem products.
  • Get more feedback from moderator interviews
    Besides users feedback via usertesting.com, we would also like to get more feedback from moderator interviews. This may include A/B testing, card sorting, and contextual inquiries.
