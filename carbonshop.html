Carbonshop

Created a One-Stop Green Shopping Platform

Overview

The average annual carbon footprint from food for a person in the United States is 2 tons, equivalent to the carbon sequestered by 7.9 acres of U.S. forests in a year. However, few grocery shopping platforms inform and encourage users to make green purchasing decisions. Therefore, we designed a mobile application that makes everyday green purchase decisions easier than ever, with diverse environmental metrics for everyday grocery consumer products and a financial incentive program that rewards green purchase decisions.

My role includes design concept initialization, user research (exploratory & in-depth), strategy & design discussions, mid-fidelity prototyping & testing, high-fidelity prototyping & testing, and final design with its validation.

Problem Statement

Carbon emissions from the grocery industry associated with agriculture, shipping, and food processing and storage are a good proxy for many environmental issues. The purchase decisions we make every day at the grocery store can truly impact the environment. However, people are unaware of the importance of low-carbon consumption in daily life, and currently there’s no efficient way to help them make daily green purchasing decisions.

Research - Key TakeAway


After synthesizing our user research results, we chose to mainly focus on these 3 pain points to generate our design strategies:

  • People need incentives in grocery shopping.
    Prices are one of the main determinants while doing grocery shopping. Also, specific rewards, including discounts, free samples, and participating in green funds, can greatly attract people to buy green groceries.
  • People find it hard to understand the importance of a low-carbon lifestyle.
    People did not purchase green products as they could not receive instant benefits through their decisions. However, current products lack carbon footprint information, and people have no idea about potential impacts.
  • People need approachable and engaging ways to participate in a low-carbon lifestyle.
    People express interests if the platform can provide an attractive way of having them join in. Also, raising awareness of environmental issues is a serious topic, and there should be approachable ways for them to understand and engage in relevant activities.

Information Architecture


With our design strategies, we started to create the information architecture.

Rationale:

  • Firstly, our application displays the users' accomplishments on the homepage to remind them of the impact they have had. Users may shop in different stores, so we integrated these stores into the homepage to help users to shop.
  • Shop is one of our main navigation because users need to see product information when shopping. Users can browse different products or search for specific products while shopping.
  • In users’ shopping carts, to draw users' attention to their impact on the environment, we will present customers with information on how much carbon emissions have been cut by their order this time compared to typical purchasing. Users can receive green points if their order has a lower carbon emissions.
  • To encourage people who are unconcerned about the environment to consider green shopping, reward plays an important role in motivating consumers to buy low-carbon goods. Users can see their environmental impact on this reward page and redeem their green points for trees or green products.
  • In the profile page, users can see their order history and their impact on the environment to summarize their purchases.

User Testing and Iteration

User Testing on UserTesting.com


To validate our final design, we conducted 12 unmoderated usability testing sessions on UserTesting.com to validate our high-fidelity prototypes. We set up a main scenario with different tasks with post-task questions to test if there are any usability issues we need to fix. We received both qualitative responses and quantitative data from the participants.

Learnings


  • Iterations
    It is important to test the prototypes to know what users think and iterate for better user flow. Through our four rounds of user testing and iterations, we learned a lot from users’ feedback and managed to design for better user flow and achieve the product goal at the same time.
  • Content writing and terminologies
    During our user testing sessions, we found that many users expressed confusion about our explanations and word choices regarding some of the features (i.e., how to redeem points). Also, we faced challenges regarding labeling products with carbon footprints and how to explain to users about our virtual points for redeeming purposes. Since one of our strategies is to inform users clearly about their potential impacts, we need to choose words carefully to avoid misinterpretation.
  • The Importance of Customization
    We found out that some users prefer to spend points to redeem goods, while others prefer to use points to redeem games. Also, it was interesting to find out people have different interpretations and understanding of carbon emissions and possible incentives. Therefore, it is important to think about motivating them in different ways.
